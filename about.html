JUNTO Core Values

🧠 Moral and Intellectual Development

Continuous growth in both ethical understanding and intellectual capacity. True wisdom integrates knowledge with moral judgment to guide thoughtful action.

"The unexamined life is not worth living, but the examined life must be lived with purpose."

🏛️ Civic Responsibility

Active engagement in democratic processes and community welfare. Citizenship requires participation, not passive observation.

"The price of freedom is eternal vigilance and active participation in the democratic process."

🔍 Free Inquiry

Unrestricted pursuit of truth through questioning assumptions and examining evidence. No topic is off-limits to honest investigation.

"Question everything, accept nothing without evidence, and remain open to changing your mind."

🕊️ Freedom and Tolerance

Defense of individual rights to think, speak, and live freely. Protecting liberty means defending those with whom we disagree.

"I may disagree with what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it."

🔐 Privacy and Security

Protection of mental and digital privacy as fundamental to human dignity. Free thought requires secure communication.

"Privacy is the foundation of human dignity and the prerequisite for authentic self-development."

🔮 Esoteric Knowledge and Raising Universal Consciousness

Exploration of hidden wisdom and consciousness expansion to elevate both individual and collective awareness.

"The mystic swims in the same waters in which the psychotic drowns - but the mystic has learned to swim."

Historical Inspiration: Benjamin Franklin's Junto

Our organization draws inspiration from Benjamin Franklin's original Junto, formed in Philadelphia in 1727. Franklin created this club for "mutual improvement," bringing together ingenious minds to discuss morals, politics, and natural philosophy in pursuit of collective wisdom.

"The Rules I drew up requir'd that every Member in his Turn should produce one or more Queries on any Point of Morals, Politics or Natural Philosophy, to be discuss'd by the Company, and once in three Months pronounce and read an Essay of his own Writing on any Subject he pleased."
— Benjamin Franklin, on the original Junto

Franklin's Four Qualifications for Membership

1
Have you any particular disrespect to any present members?
Answer: I have not.
2
Do you sincerely declare that you love mankind in general; of what profession or religion soever?
Answer: I do.
3
Do you think any person ought to be harmed in his body, name or goods, for mere speculative opinions, or his external way of worship?
Answer: No.
4
Do you love truth for truth's sake, and will you endeavour impartially to find and receive it yourself and communicate it to others?
Answer: Yes.
Franklin's Junto embodied the same principles we hold today: the pursuit of truth, civic responsibility, mutual improvement, and the betterment of society through enlightened discourse. Like our predecessors, we gather to challenge assumptions, expand knowledge, and work toward the common good.

Franklin's Standing Queries for the Junto

These 24 questions were read and answered at every meeting of Franklin's original Junto. They guided discussions toward practical wisdom, community service, and mutual improvement. Many remain remarkably relevant to our modern pursuits.

Knowledge & Learning (1-2)

1. Have you met with any thing in the author you last read, remarkable, or suitable to be communicated to the Junto?
2. What new story have you lately heard agreeable for telling in conversation?

Business & Economics (3-5)

3. Hath any citizen in your knowledge failed in his business lately, and what have you heard of the cause?
4. Have you lately heard of any citizen's thriving well, and by what means?
5. Have you lately heard how any present rich man, here or elsewhere, got his estate?

Moral Conduct & Community (6-8)

6. Do you know of any fellow citizen, who has lately done a worthy action, deserving praise and imitation?
7. What unhappy effects of intemperance have you lately observed or heard? of imprudence? of passion?
8. What happy effects of temperance? of prudence? of moderation? or of any other virtue?

Civic & Legal Matters (14-15, 23)

14. Have you lately observed any defect in the laws of your country, which it would be proper to move the legislature for an amendment?
15. Have you lately observed any encroachment on the just liberties of the people?
23. Is there any difficulty in matters of opinion, of justice, and injustice, which you would gladly have discussed at this time?

Mutual Aid & Service (11-13, 16-22)

11. Do you think of any thing at present, in which the Junto may be serviceable to mankind? to their country, to their friends, or to themselves?
12. Hath any deserving stranger arrived in town since last meeting, that you heard of?
20. In what manner can the Junto, or any of them, assist you in any of your honourable designs?
24. Do you see any thing amiss in the present customs or proceedings of the Junto, which might be amended?
These questions reveal Franklin's genius: they systematically directed attention toward learning, virtue, community service, and practical wisdom. They transformed casual conversation into purposeful discourse aimed at mutual improvement and social betterment.
"An investment in knowledge pays the best interest."
— Benjamin Franklin
